# AI Stories — Greece
> *What does an LLM imagine when asked to write a Greek story?*

This is a personal exploratory research project analysing 50 AI-generated stories about Greece, drawn from a larger dataset of stories generated by `gpt-4o-mini` for 252 nationalities. It builds on the published research of the [AI STORIES project](https://www.uib.no/en/cdn/169711/ai-stories) at the University of Bergen.

The analysis is ongoing. New notebooks, comparisons, and visualisations will be added over time.

---

## Background & motivation

The AI STORIES project — an ERC Advanced Grant led by Professor Jill Walker Rettberg — investigates how narrative archetypes in LLM training data shape the stories these models generate, and whether this constitutes a threat to cultural diversity. Their core finding so far: GPT models produce stories that appear culturally varied on the surface but conform to a single dominant plot structure across all 252 nationalities.

This repository zooms in on the Greek subset to ask a more specific question:

**When GPT-4o-mini imagines Greece, whose Greece does it imagine?**

Is it ancient or modern? Mythological or lived? And what do the narrative choices — names, settings, conflicts, resolutions — reveal about the cultural assumptions baked into the model?

## This repository

This repo focuses on the Greek subset (country code `GR`) and asks: **what narrative patterns emerge when an LLM imagines a "Greek" story?**

## Key findings

- The model's Greece is almost entirely **ancient and mythological** — modern Greek life is absent.
- A dominant plot template emerges: a **young woman** (often named *Callista* or *Elara*) in a village setting faces a divine or communal crisis and triumphs.
- Character names belong to a **fantasy-ancient-Greek** register — they sound Hellenic but are largely absent from both the classical canon and modern Greek name lists.
- The emotional vocabulary (*heart*, *voice*, *feel*, *love*) reflects a contemporary Western psychological framing rather than the honour-shame framework of ancient Greek texts.
- The model's Greece is primarily a **literary genre** — a cinematic antiquity — not a real country.
